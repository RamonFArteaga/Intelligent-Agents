Critical Reflection Final Assignment

This section presents three formative reflective case studies. Which are based on themes explored during the module and relate directly to my practical work, design decisions, and observations made while developing and testing agent-based systems.

Case #1: Bias in autonomous decision-making.

Case #2: Explainable decisions in agents (XAI)

Reflective Commentary: Development of Inquiro

Introduction

Inquiro is an LLM-powered multi-agent academic research system that decomposes a research question, retrieves papers from arXiv, validates results, summarizes and ranks evidence, and produces a structured report. The proposal was developed collaboratively by my team, then I completed the coding, integration, testing, modifications and final documentation individually as an assignment. The team discussions helped define the original four-agent architecture, but individual implementation exposed gaps that were not evident during the proposal stage. I therefore added a fifth agent, the Validator, plus logging, performance measurement, automated testing and controlled error handling. Using Rolfe, Freshwater and Jasper’s (2001) What?–So what?–Now what? framework, I reflect on bias, explainability and responsible deployment.

Case Study 1: Bias in Autonomous Decision-Making

What?

The Processor Agent used Mistral to summarize papers and assign relevance scores from 0 to 100. Initially, I treated this ranking as a technical output and assumed that papers from an academic database would be assessed objectively. During testing, I recognized that the ranking depended on the query wording, the papers available in arXiv and the model’s interpretation. The apparent precision of a numerical score could therefore conceal uncertainty.

So what?

This changed my understanding of bias. I had associated AI bias mainly with unfair decisions involving people, but Inquiro showed me that bias can also emerge through source selection, terminology, database coverage and scoring criteria. A relevant paper could rank lower because it used different language, while a less important paper could score highly because its abstract closely matched the query. Retrieval-Augmented Generation grounds outputs in external evidence (Lewis et al., 2020); however, my testing demonstrated that grounding does not make either retrieval or model judgement neutral. I also realized that my design decisions shaped the outcome. Selecting only arXiv was practical and cost-effective, but it restricted the evidence base. This made me more cautious about presenting the ranking as authoritative and showed me that autonomous decisions must be evaluated across the whole pipeline.

Now what?

I would retrieve from multiple databases, compare rankings across models and ask human reviewers to assess samples. I would label relevance scores as model-generated recommendations and expose the criteria behind them. This learning applies directly to my industrial work at JBS, where predictive systems may influence maintenance priorities. I would not permit a model score alone to determine a high-risk maintenance decision.

Case Study 2: Explainable Decisions in Agents

What?

The proposal identified intermediate outputs as support for explainability. In the final system, the Planner generated subtasks, the Processor provided score justifications, and the workflow stored logs and metrics. I also used a deterministic Jinja2 template for the Writer instead of allowing another LLM to rewrite the report. This reduced the risk of unsupported claims and improved consistency.

So what?

These features helped me distinguish transparency from explainability. A reasoning trace or justification shows what the system produced, but it does not prove that the reasoning is correct. LLM explanations can sound persuasive despite incomplete evidence. Yao et al. (2023) demonstrate the value of combining reasoning and action, but my implementation showed that observable reasoning still requires verification.

I felt more confident when logs made the workflow visible because errors became easier to trace. However, I also became concerned that users could over-trust fluent explanations. My industrial troubleshooting experience influenced this response. A maintenance diagnosis must be verified against measurements, equipment condition and manuals; plausibility alone is insufficient. The same principle applies to agents.

Now what?

I would show which abstract passages influenced each score and separate retrieved facts from model interpretation. I would retain deterministic reporting, logs and source links as an audit trail. In future systems, explanations will support human judgement rather than replace it. I now view explainability as a verification mechanism, not merely a presentation feature.

Case Study 3: Responsible Deployment of Agents

What?

Important changes resulted from failure. During testing, arXiv returned an HTTP 503 error, while Mistral sometimes produced malformed JSON or additional text. I added controlled error handling, JSON extraction, fallback behavior and a Validator Agent to remove duplicate or incomplete records. The final system also completed five automated tests and recorded execution metrics.

So what?

These incidents changed my focus from “Can the system complete the task?” to “Can it fail safely and visibly?” Russell and Norvig (2021) describe agents through goal-directed action, but responsible deployment also requires limits on that action. Validation prevented poor-quality data from reaching semantic processing, while graceful degradation prevented an external failure from causing an uncontrolled crash. The teamwork stage also affected me emotionally. I felt disappointed because some members did not appear to contribute ideas or progress consistently. My role as a maintenance superintendent at JBS has trained me to work under pressure, so I remained focused and ensured that the proposal was delivered on time. Nevertheless, I felt that I was carrying more responsibility than expected. My response was to take control rather than establish stronger accountability early. This protected the deadline but limited collaboration. I was also disappointed with the grade because I believed the work demonstrated greater effort and technical quality. Reflecting on this, I recognize that effort must be made visible through documented decisions, contributions and evidence.

Now what?

In future team projects, I will establish responsibilities, intermediate deadlines and contribution records from the beginning. For agent deployment, I will include validation, testing, logging, human approval and recovery behavior in the initial architecture. Wooldridge (2009) emphasizes defined agent responsibilities; I now understand that human responsibilities must be equally explicit.

Conclusion

Inquiro developed my technical skills in LangGraph orchestration, local LLM integration, validation, testing, error handling and deterministic reporting. More importantly, it changed how I evaluate intelligent agents. I now examine bias across the entire pipeline, treat explanations as evidence that requires verification, and design systems for safe failure, transparency and human oversight. The project also revealed a weakness in my teamwork behavior: under pressure, I tend to assume control rather than delegate. In the future, I will combine technical ownership with clearer responsibilities, earlier communication, documented contributions and stronger collaboration, while applying these lessons to academic projects and industrial AI systems.

Project Proposal

This section presents the original proposal for Inquiro, an LLM-powered multi-agent academic research system developed as part of the Artificial Intelligence Agents module. The proposal describes the initial architecture, design decisions, development methodology, and technology stack that guided the implementation of the final system.

1. Executive Summary

Inquiro is a proposed LLM-powered multi-agent system designed to support academic research. The system decomposes research questions into manageable subtasks, retrieves relevant literature, processes research findings, and generates structured offline reports.

Rather than relying on a single monolithic AI model, the proposal introduces multiple specialised agents coordinated through LangGraph. This architecture improves modularity, transparency, maintainability, and simplifies future system expansion.

2. Literature Review and Academic Context

The proposal is based on established research in intelligent agents, multi-agent systems and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG).

  • Russell and Norvig describe intelligent agents as autonomous systems capable of perceiving their environment and acting toward specific goals.
  • Wooldridge explains that Multi-Agent Systems distribute responsibilities among specialised agents, improving modularity and maintainability.
  • Lewis et al. introduce Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), reducing hallucinations by grounding LLM responses using external knowledge sources.
  • Yao et al. demonstrate the ReAct methodology, combining reasoning and action within Large Language Model agents.

These concepts formed the theoretical foundation of the proposed Inquiro architecture.

3. System Requirements and Assumptions

Functional Requirements

  • Accept research queries from the user.
  • Decompose complex research topics into smaller subtasks.
  • Retrieve relevant academic literature.
  • Summarise, filter and rank research findings.
  • Generate structured research reports.
  • Store reports locally for future reference.

Project Assumptions

  • Internet connectivity is available.
  • Academic APIs remain accessible.
  • Free-tier Large Language Models are sufficient for a prototype.

4. Design Decisions and Critical Evaluation

Several architectural decisions were made during the proposal phase to maximize modularity, maintainability and reproducibility.

LangGraph

LangGraph was selected because it provides explicit state management, deterministic workflows and simplifies communication between independent agents.

Multi-Agent Architecture

A four-agent architecture was selected instead of a single-agent solution. Separating responsibilities between specialised agents simplifies testing, maintenance and future scalability.

Mistral-7B

Mistral-7B was proposed as the primary language model due to its open-source availability, strong instruction-following capabilities and ability to run locally through Ollama.

arXiv

The arXiv repository was selected as the primary academic knowledge source because it provides high-quality scientific literature without subscription fees.

Jinja2 Templates

Instead of generating reports entirely through LLM responses, deterministic Jinja2 templates were proposed to improve consistency, repeatability and reproducibility of generated reports.

5. System Architecture and Coordination Topology

The proposed architecture consists of four specialised agents coordinated through a shared LangGraph state object.

  • Planner Agent — Decomposes complex research questions into structured subtasks.
  • Retriever Agent — Retrieves relevant academic literature from arXiv and optional web sources.
  • Processor Agent — Summarises, ranks and removes duplicate information.
  • Writer Agent — Produces a structured Markdown research report and stores it locally.

All agents communicate exclusively through a shared LangGraph state object, allowing deterministic workflow execution while maintaining loose coupling between system components.

System Architecture

The proposed Inquiro architecture was designed around four specialised agents coordinated through a LangGraph orchestrator. Each agent performs a single responsibility while sharing information through a common state object, allowing deterministic execution and modular system expansion.

The overall workflow follows this sequence:

  1. User submits a research question.
  2. LangGraph initializes and manages the shared workflow state.
  3. The Planner Agent decomposes the research problem into smaller subtasks.
  4. The Retriever Agent searches academic repositories.
  5. The Processor Agent evaluates, summarizes and ranks retrieved papers.
  6. The Writer Agent generates a structured Markdown report.
  7. The report is stored locally for future access.

8. Challenges and Mitigation Strategies

  • Hallucinations: Mitigated through Retrieval-Augmented Generation and grounding in external academic sources.
  • Task decomposition errors: Reduced through structured prompts and clearly defined output formats.
  • Noisy or duplicate results: Filtered through ranking, similarity comparison and deduplication.
  • Limited explainability: Addressed by exposing intermediate outputs generated by each agent.
  • API failures: Mitigated through retries, caching and fallback retrieval sources.
  • Agent coordination: Managed through LangGraph state tracking and deterministic workflow routing.

9. Evaluation Strategy

The proposed system was intended to be evaluated using predefined research questions of varying complexity.

The evaluation metrics included:

  • Relevance of the retrieved academic information.
  • Completeness of the generated report.
  • Coherence and readability of the final output.
  • Total processing time.
  • User satisfaction.

Independent evaluation by multiple team members was proposed to reduce individual bias. Success would be measured against the assignment criteria, including data retrieval, data processing and offline storage of usable outputs.

11. Conclusion

This proposal outlined the design of Inquiro, an LLM-powered multi-agent system intended to support academic research and information gathering. The proposed solution used a Planner Agent, Retriever Agent, Processor Agent and Writer Agent working together to decompose research questions, retrieve relevant information, process findings and generate structured reports.

The design was developed using concepts studied throughout the module, including intelligent agents, task decomposition and agent collaboration. A multi-agent approach was selected because it separates complex research tasks into smaller, more manageable components, making the system easier to understand, test and develop.

The proposed technology stack relied on freely available tools such as LangGraph, Hugging Face, Ollama and the arXiv API, making the solution practical to implement within the scope of the project.

Although challenges related to retrieval accuracy, explainability and source quality remained, appropriate mitigation strategies were identified as part of the design.

Overall, the proposal demonstrated how agent-based approaches and Large Language Models could be combined to support academic research tasks in a structured and practical way.

Agent Dialogue Using KQML and KIF

Activity Overview

This activity illustrates structured communication between two autonomous software agents through the combined use of Knowledge Query and Manipulation Language (KQML) and Knowledge Interchange Format (KIF). Alice is a procurement agent responsible for evaluating stock requirements and supporting purchasing decisions. Bob is a warehouse inventory agent responsible for monitoring stock availability and maintaining product-specification data.

KQML expresses the communicative intention of each message, while KIF represents the semantic content of the information being requested or supplied.

Message 1: Alice Queries the Available Stock

(ask-one
    :sender Alice
    :receiver Bob
    :language KIF
    :ontology warehouse-inventory
    :reply-with stock-query-001
    :content
        "(available-stock
            (television
                (screen-size 50-inch))
            ?quantity)")

Alice initiates the interaction using the KQML performative ask-one. This performative is appropriate because she is requesting a single determinate value: the number of 50-inch televisions currently available in the warehouse. The variable ?quantity represents the unknown value that Bob is expected to resolve.

Message 2: Bob Reports the Stock Quantity

(tell
    :sender Bob
    :receiver Alice
    :language KIF
    :ontology warehouse-inventory
    :in-reply-to stock-query-001
    :content
        "(available-stock
            (television
                (screen-size 50-inch))
            24)")

Bob responds with the KQML performative tell, indicating that he is communicating a proposition that he holds to be true. The KIF expression states that the warehouse currently contains 24 available 50-inch televisions. The in-reply-to field preserves the relationship between the response and Alice's original query.

Message 3: Alice Queries the Number of HDMI Ports

(ask-one
    :sender Alice
    :receiver Bob
    :language KIF
    :ontology product-specifications
    :reply-with hdmi-query-001
    :content
        "(hdmi-port-count
            (television
                (screen-size 50-inch))
            ?number-of-ports)")

Alice submits a second query concerning the technical characteristics of the televisions. The ontology changes from warehouse-inventory to product-specifications, reflecting the distinction between stock-level information and product-feature information.

Message 4: Bob Provides the HDMI-Port Specification

(tell
    :sender Bob
    :receiver Alice
    :language KIF
    :ontology product-specifications
    :in-reply-to hdmi-query-001
    :content
        "(hdmi-port-count
            (television
                (screen-size 50-inch))
            3)")

Bob replies that each 50-inch television contains three HDMI ports. The message remains machine-interpretable because the communicative act is represented separately from the factual content. KQML identifies the message as an assertion, while KIF encodes the product specification.

Message 5: Alice Confirms Receipt of the Information

(tell
    :sender Alice
    :receiver Bob
    :language KIF
    :ontology procurement
    :content
        "(information-received
            (available-stock
                (television
                    (screen-size 50-inch))
                24)
            (hdmi-port-count
                (television
                    (screen-size 50-inch))
                3))")

Alice confirms that both the inventory data and the technical specification have been received successfully. This information can then support a procurement decision, such as determining whether the current stock level is sufficient or whether additional units should be ordered.

Critical Discussion

The dialogue demonstrates how agent communication languages support coordination between autonomous and functionally specialised software entities. Alice and Bob do not invoke one another's internal procedures directly. Instead, they communicate through structured messages that explicitly represent both communicative intent and semantic content.

KQML provides the interaction protocol through performatives such as ask-one and tell. These performatives distinguish between requesting information and asserting information. This distinction is significant because agent communication is not limited to transferring data; it also represents the intended computational function of each message.

KIF complements KQML by providing a formal representation of the knowledge being exchanged. In this case, that knowledge concerns product type, screen size, stock quantity and HDMI-port count. Variables such as ?quantity and ?number-of-ports support declarative queries, while the returned values convert those queries into explicit propositions.

The use of separate ontologies improves semantic precision. Inventory availability is represented within the warehouse-inventory ontology, whereas technical features are represented within the product-specifications ontology. This reduces ambiguity and supports interoperability between agents that may have been implemented independently.

From an architectural perspective, the scenario illustrates the value of distributed responsibility. Alice is responsible for procurement reasoning, while Bob maintains authoritative knowledge of warehouse stock and product attributes. Such separation improves modularity, maintainability and organisational scalability because each agent can evolve independently while continuing to communicate through an agreed protocol.

Successful interaction nevertheless depends on both agents sharing compatible assumptions regarding vocabulary, ontology and message structure. Although KQML identifies the intention of a message, it does not independently guarantee that both agents interpret the underlying concepts identically. Robust multi-agent systems therefore require clearly defined ontologies, validation mechanisms and suitable handling of incomplete, inconsistent or unavailable information.

Relationship to the Learning Outcomes

This activity demonstrates an understanding of the motivations for agent-based computing by showing how autonomous agents can distribute responsibilities, maintain separate knowledge domains and coordinate through explicit communication.

It also reflects principles underlying contemporary agent models. Alice and Bob exhibit goal-directed behaviour, role specialisation and structured interaction. Their communication is grounded in established artificial intelligence research concerning knowledge representation, speech-act theory and multi-agent coordination.

Constituency-Based Parse Trees

1. The government raised interest rates.

(S
  (NP
    (DT The)
    (NN government))
  (VP
    (VBD raised)
    (NP
      (NN interest)
      (NNS rates)))
  (. .))

This sentence has a straightforward constituent structure. The subject noun phrase is The government, while the verb phrase consists of the verb raised and the direct-object noun phrase interest rates.

2. The internet gives everyone a voice.

(S
  (NP
    (DT The)
    (NN internet))
  (VP
    (VBZ gives)
    (NP
      (PRP everyone))
    (NP
      (DT a)
      (NN voice)))
  (. .))

This sentence contains a ditransitive verb structure. The noun phrase everyone functions as the indirect object, while a voice functions as the direct object.

3. The man saw the dog with the telescope.

This sentence is syntactically ambiguous because the prepositional phrase with the telescope can attach either to the verb phrase or to the noun phrase.

Interpretation A: The Man Used the Telescope

(S
  (NP
    (DT The)
    (NN man))
  (VP
    (VBD saw)
    (NP
      (DT the)
      (NN dog))
    (PP
      (IN with)
      (NP
        (DT the)
        (NN telescope))))
  (. .))

In this interpretation, the prepositional phrase modifies the verb saw. The telescope is therefore understood as the instrument used by the man.

Interpretation B: The Dog Was Associated with the Telescope

(S
  (NP
    (DT The)
    (NN man))
  (VP
    (VBD saw)
    (NP
      (NP
        (DT the)
        (NN dog))
      (PP
        (IN with)
        (NP
          (DT the)
          (NN telescope)))))
  (. .))

In this interpretation, the prepositional phrase modifies the dog. The sentence therefore implies that the dog is associated with or carrying the telescope.

Critical Discussion

These parse trees demonstrate how constituency-based analysis represents the hierarchical structure of natural-language sentences. Rather than treating a sentence as a flat sequence of words, constituency parsing groups related elements into syntactic units such as noun phrases, verb phrases and prepositional phrases.

The third example is particularly significant because it illustrates prepositional-phrase attachment ambiguity, a recurrent challenge in natural-language processing. Both interpretations are grammatically valid, but they convey different meanings. An intelligent system must therefore use semantic information, contextual evidence or probabilistic models to determine the most plausible interpretation.

This issue is relevant to intelligent agents because language-enabled agents must do more than identify grammatical structure. They must also resolve ambiguity before taking action, retrieving information or generating a response. Incorrect syntactic interpretation could lead to inaccurate reasoning or inappropriate behaviour.

Relationship to the Learning Outcomes

The activity develops the knowledge and practical skills needed to apply natural-language processing techniques within intelligent systems. It also highlights a contemporary research issue in intelligent agent systems: the reliable interpretation of ambiguous human language.

Deep Learning Application: Industrial Predictive Maintenance

Overview of the Technology

One deep learning application likely to have a substantial societal and industrial impact is predictive maintenance for critical machinery. Predictive maintenance uses operational data to estimate equipment condition, identify abnormal behaviour and anticipate failure before a breakdown occurs. In industrial environments, this may include monitoring motors, pumps, compressors, gearboxes, conveyors and production-line equipment.

Unlike reactive maintenance, where repairs are completed after failure, or preventive maintenance, where components are replaced at predetermined intervals, predictive maintenance attempts to determine when intervention is genuinely required. Deep learning is particularly relevant because industrial equipment produces large volumes of time-series data from vibration sensors, temperature probes, electrical measurements, pressure transmitters and supervisory control systems.

How It Works

A deep learning predictive-maintenance system begins by collecting historical and real-time sensor data. These data may include motor current, vibration, bearing temperature, oil pressure, speed, load and previous failure records.

The data are cleaned, normalised and divided into time windows before being supplied to a deep neural network. Different architectures may be selected according to the maintenance problem:

  • Convolutional neural networks can extract local patterns from vibration signals or transformed sensor data.
  • Long short-term memory and gated recurrent unit networks can model changes occurring across time.
  • Autoencoders can learn normal equipment behaviour and identify deviations as anomalies.
  • Hybrid models can combine feature extraction with failure classification or remaining-useful-life prediction.

The model produces an output such as a failure probability, anomaly score, equipment-health classification or estimate of remaining useful life. A maintenance agent could then combine this result with operational priorities, production schedules and maintenance resources. However, the model should support human decision-making rather than autonomously authorising high-risk maintenance actions.

Potential Positive Impact

Predictive maintenance could provide significant social and organisational benefits. Earlier detection of mechanical deterioration may prevent sudden equipment failures that could injure employees, damage machinery or interrupt essential production. It may also reduce unnecessary component replacement, energy inefficiency and material waste.

This is particularly relevant to my industrial experience. In a processing facility, motor current, vibration, temperature and VFD information can reveal developing mechanical or electrical problems. A deep learning model could analyse these signals continuously and prioritise assets requiring inspection. The practical value would not simply be predicting failure; it would be converting equipment data into timely and explainable maintenance decisions.

Predictive maintenance may also improve workforce effectiveness. Maintenance technicians could spend less time responding to avoidable emergencies and more time conducting planned inspections, root-cause analysis and reliability improvements.

Ethical and Socio-Technical Concerns

Despite these benefits, predictive maintenance presents several ethical and operational risks. The first is automation bias. A numerical failure probability may appear objective even when it is based on incomplete sensor data, an unrepresentative training set or changing operating conditions. Employees may therefore defer to the model even when physical inspection suggests a different conclusion.

False negatives are particularly serious because an undetected failure could expose employees to unsafe equipment. False positives may also be harmful because they can cause unnecessary shutdowns, wasted labour and avoidable replacement costs. Data quality is therefore central to system reliability.

There are also privacy and workforce-surveillance concerns. Equipment data may indirectly record operator behaviour, production performance or individual working patterns. If such data are repurposed for employee evaluation without transparency or consent, a maintenance system could become a mechanism for workplace monitoring.

Another concern is accountability. Responsibility must remain clearly defined when a model recommends maintenance but a manager, engineer or technician decides whether to act. Organisations should not use an opaque model to transfer responsibility away from human decision-makers.

Deep learning models may also be difficult to interpret. A system may correctly identify an abnormal condition without clearly explaining which physical signals influenced the result. In a safety-critical environment, an unexplained alert is less useful than one that identifies the affected asset, relevant sensor trends, confidence level and probable failure mode.

Responsible Deployment

A responsible implementation should include:

  • Validation against real equipment failures and maintenance records.
  • Continuous monitoring for model drift and sensor deterioration.
  • Clear confidence levels and explanations.
  • Human verification before shutdown or repair decisions.
  • Audit logs showing model outputs and human actions.
  • Access controls governing operational and employee-related data.
  • Fallback procedures when sensors, networks or models fail.

Conclusion

Deep learning predictive maintenance has the potential to improve industrial safety, reliability, resource efficiency and operational continuity. Its greatest value lies in identifying complex deterioration patterns that may not be visible through conventional inspections or fixed alarm thresholds.

However, successful deployment requires more than model accuracy. The system must operate within a socio-technical framework that includes reliable sensors, competent technicians, transparent decision processes, cybersecurity, human oversight and clearly assigned accountability. In my view, predictive maintenance should function as an intelligent decision-support capability rather than an autonomous replacement for engineering judgement.

3. Agent Responsibilities

3.1 Planner Agent

The Planner Agent receives the research question and creates a structured execution plan.

It produces:

  • A reasoning trace.
  • A list of research subtasks.
  • A planning output used by the remaining workflow.

The Planner Agent does not answer the research question directly. This design demonstrates planning behaviour and improves transparency.

3.2 Retriever Agent

The Retriever Agent searches the arXiv academic repository.

It retrieves:

  • Paper titles.
  • Authors.
  • Abstracts.
  • Publication dates.
  • Source URLs.

The Retriever Agent also checks whether the user query appears to be an academic research question before sending it to arXiv.

3.3 Validator Agent

The Validator Agent performs data-quality checks before semantic processing.

It:

  • Removes duplicate papers.
  • Removes incomplete records.
  • Confirms that required metadata is present.
  • Prevents malformed data from reaching the Processor Agent.

The Validator Agent was added during development as an improvement over the original four-agent proposal.

3.4 Processor Agent

The Processor Agent uses the local Mistral model to analyse retrieved papers.

It generates:

  • A concise summary for each paper.
  • A relevance score between 0 and 100.
  • A justification for the score.
  • A ranking of papers from highest to lowest relevance.

All papers are processed in a single LLM request to reduce execution time and improve ranking consistency.

3.5 Writer Agent

The Writer Agent generates the final Markdown report.

It uses a deterministic Jinja2 template instead of asking the LLM to write the complete report.

This design improves:

  • Consistency.
  • Reproducibility.
  • Testability.
  • Report structure.
  • Control over the final output.

The Writer Agent does not introduce new claims. It assembles the information produced by previous agents.

Critical Evaluation of the Final Project

A critical evaluation of Inquiro, comparing the Week 7 design proposal with the Week 11 implemented solution across architectural, technical, evaluative and ethical dimensions.

1. Introduction

This critical evaluation examines the development of Inquiro, an LLM-powered multi-agent academic research system. It compares the design produced during Week 7 with the implemented solution completed in Week 11. The purpose is not simply to describe the system, but to evaluate the quality of the original design decisions, the extent to which implementation objectives were achieved, the significance of deviations from the proposal, and the broader technical and socio-technical implications of the final solution.

The Week 7 design proposed four specialised agents: Planner, Retriever, Processor and Writer. These were coordinated through LangGraph and supported by arXiv, Mistral, Ollama, Jinja2 and Streamlit. By Week 11, the system had evolved into a five-agent architecture through the addition of a Validator Agent, alongside structured JSON handling, execution logging, performance metrics, automated testing, controlled error handling and deterministic report generation.

2. Evaluation of the Week 7 Design Phase

2.1 Strengths of the Original Design

The Week 7 proposal demonstrated a sound understanding of modular agent-based architecture. Dividing the workflow into specialised agents reduced the complexity associated with a monolithic language model and supported clearer separation of responsibilities. This improved maintainability, testability and the ability to reason about system behaviour.

The selection of LangGraph was appropriate because the project required explicit state management and controlled transitions between agent stages. This was preferable to an informal chain of prompts, because it allowed intermediate outputs to be preserved and the execution sequence to remain observable.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation was also a strong design choice. Grounding the system in arXiv literature reduced the risk of unsupported academic claims. The use of Jinja2 for deterministic report generation was similarly well justified because it separated evidence processing from document formatting and reduced uncontrolled text generation.

The proposed technology stack was realistic and cost-conscious. Open-source components and local model execution improved reproducibility and reduced dependence on proprietary services.

2.2 Limitations of the Original Design

The original four-agent architecture underestimated the importance of validation. It assumed that retrieved information could pass directly into semantic processing, despite common issues such as duplicate papers, missing abstracts, malformed metadata and temporary API failures.

The proposal also treated explainability primarily as visibility of intermediate outputs. Although this improves transparency, it does not prove that the reasoning is valid or complete. A stronger design would have distinguished between transparency, interpretability and verifiability.

The proposed evaluation criteria, including relevance, completeness, coherence, processing time and user satisfaction, were appropriate but insufficiently operationalised. The proposal did not define precise thresholds, scoring procedures or baseline comparisons, which limited the potential strength of the evaluation.

Dependence on arXiv also narrowed the evidence base. This simplified implementation but introduced source bias and risked excluding relevant peer-reviewed research from other databases.

3. Evaluation of the Week 11 Implemented Solution

3.1 Architectural Improvements

The addition of the Validator Agent was the most significant architectural improvement. It introduced an explicit stage for checking completeness, removing duplicates and filtering unsuitable records before processing. This corrected a genuine weakness in the original design and strengthened separation of concerns.

The final five-agent architecture provided clearer contracts between stages. The Planner decomposed the task, the Retriever acquired evidence, the Validator assessed input quality, the Processor summarised and ranked findings, and the Writer produced the final deterministic report.

Logging, performance metrics and structured state improved observability. These features made failures easier to diagnose and shifted the project from a conceptual agent demonstration toward a more auditable software system.

3.2 Implementation Effectiveness

The implemented system successfully achieved the central objective of the Week 7 proposal. It demonstrated autonomous task decomposition, academic information retrieval, validation, semantic processing and structured report generation.

Deterministic report generation was particularly effective. Allowing unrestricted final-stage generation would have increased the risk of unsupported claims. Jinja2 constrained the Writer Agent and preserved stronger traceability between retrieved evidence and the final report.

Automated testing also strengthened the implementation by providing evidence of repeatability. However, the test set remained small, so it offered limited evidence about generalisability across disciplines, query types and failure conditions.

3.3 Robustness and Failure Management

The implementation encountered realistic failures, including arXiv HTTP 503 errors and malformed JSON produced by the local language model. The introduction of controlled exceptions, fallback behaviour, JSON extraction and validation was technically appropriate.

These improvements enabled graceful degradation rather than uncontrolled failure. Nevertheless, a production-grade system would require stronger retry policies, caching, source redundancy, health monitoring and recovery from partial state corruption.

4. Comparison Between Design and Implementation

The final implementation remained faithful to the central vision of the Week 7 proposal. The principles of modularity, local model execution, academic retrieval and deterministic reporting were preserved.

The principal divergence was the addition of the Validator Agent and the stronger emphasis on reliability engineering. This was not a failure of design consistency; it was evidence of iterative refinement based on implementation experience.

Explainability also evolved. The proposal focused on displaying intermediate outputs, while the final implementation added logs, metrics and explicit validation. This represented a movement from presentational transparency toward operational accountability.

5. Evaluation Methodology and Evidence

The project included automated tests and performance measurements, providing evidence of basic reliability and repeatability. However, the evaluation would have been stronger with a formal baseline.

A comparison with a single-agent implementation or a conventional retrieval pipeline would have helped determine whether the multi-agent design produced measurable improvements in quality, accuracy or maintainability.

Relevance scoring should also have been assessed by independent human reviewers. Model-generated relevance scores cannot serve as their own validation standard.

A larger and more diverse test set should have included ambiguous queries, interdisciplinary topics, failed retrievals and adversarial prompts. Formal error analysis would also have helped identify where and why the system performed poorly.

6. Ethical and Socio-Technical Evaluation

Inquiro presents a lower level of physical risk than an industrial control agent, but it still raises important ethical concerns. The system ranks evidence and generates reports that may influence users' understanding of a research topic.

Source bias is central. Restricting retrieval to arXiv may privilege particular disciplines and publication cultures. The ranking process may reinforce this bias by favouring papers whose abstracts closely resemble the wording of the query.

Automation bias is also relevant. Numerical relevance scores and fluent summaries may encourage users to accept the system's judgement without checking the original papers. Outputs should therefore be presented as recommendations rather than authoritative conclusions.

Academic integrity must also be considered. The system can support literature discovery and synthesis, but it should not replace critical reading or original academic writing. Source links, provenance and disclosure of model involvement should remain visible.

7. Professional and Personal Reflection

The project demonstrated that a technically plausible architecture can reveal unanticipated weaknesses during implementation. The most important learning was that validation, observability and controlled failure are core architectural requirements rather than optional additions.

It also demonstrated the difference between producing a coherent output and establishing confidence in that output. A report may appear credible while still containing ranking bias, incomplete evidence or model-generated error. This reinforced the importance of verification and human oversight.

From a teamwork perspective, the proposal stage demonstrated the value of collaborative planning, but the implementation exposed limitations in responsibility allocation and contribution tracking. Future projects should establish explicit roles, milestones, decision ownership and documented evidence of contribution from the outset.

8. Recommendations for Future Development

  • Integrate multiple academic databases.
  • Introduce independent human relevance assessment.
  • Benchmark against single-agent and non-agent baselines.
  • Add citation-level provenance for generated claims.
  • Display confidence and uncertainty indicators.
  • Expand adversarial and failure-oriented testing.
  • Implement stronger retries, caching and source redundancy.
  • Conduct usability testing with students or researchers.
  • Add explicit academic-integrity guidance.

9. Overall Judgement

Overall, Inquiro was a successful implementation of the Week 7 proposal and demonstrated a credible application of agent-based computing to academic research support. The project showed strong architectural reasoning, appropriate use of specialised agents, effective retrieval grounding and meaningful refinement during implementation.

Its principal limitations were the small evaluation scale, dependence on a single academic source, incomplete benchmarking and the absence of formal human assessment. These limitations restrict the strength of claims that can be made regarding accuracy, generalisability and superiority over simpler approaches.

The most important achievement was the evolution from a theoretically modular design into a more robust and accountable implementation. The addition of validation, logging, testing and graceful degradation demonstrated critical engagement with the difference between a prototype that works under ideal conditions and an intelligent system designed to operate responsibly under realistic conditions.
